About

“What do you want to do when you grow up?” was a question I had trouble answering. In many people’s eyes, my love of art and literature contradicted my knack for math and computer science.

In my first software engineering internship last summer, I happened to sit in on a UX design working session, and I found a place where these disparate parts of my life came together: the visual thinking acquired from years of designing graphics and making art, the empathy accumulated from reading broadly, and the front-end technical skills that help me understand the implementation of UI elements.

I care deeply about making software accessible and delightful to use. It’s not something that could be brushed aside the way some engineers do; good design helps a product fulfill its purpose and expand its impact.

This is what I want to do.

My co-leader of this project, Lexi Ryan, could not remember the names and relationships of the hundreds of relatives at her family reunion. We decided to make an app to help people remember who their relatives are and how they’re related.

Research

It’s 2019. Families no longer fit in the cookie-cutter mold consisting of a husband, a wife, and their children. Around a third of American children are living with an unmarried parent. Around 7 million Americans are adopted. Same-sex couples are entitled to marry in the same way as heterosexual ones, with full parental rights.

Figure 1: Screen shot from the app Family Tree

Figure 1 detail

The existing apps for keeping track of family relations focus on biological relations and genealogy. For example, the app Family Tree (Figure 1) only allows users to add their father and mother, placing strict restrictions on the number and gender of parents. Someone might be the adopted child of a same-sex couple, but this app would not acknowledge this relationship. Alternatively, a person might have both biological parents and step-parents who are active in their life, but they must fill their biological parents into the boxes, as if they are still married. It’s easy to imagine how such a design would make many uncomfortable.

I explored the question: How might we help people keep track of their family relations, no matter what their family looks like?

Given our focus on inclusivity, I needed to come up with a system flexible enough to handle relationships of many kinds, such as same-sex relationships, adoptive relationships, and step relationships. I decided to test my designs on the fictional family in the TV show Modern Family because people might be sensitive about their family relationships.

Figure 2: Visualizing how a family tree for Modern Family characters might look like.

Figure 3: This stumped me.

Traditionally, families are thought of as trees. However, divorces, remarriages, and adoptions make it difficult to represent a family as a 2D diagram (Figure 3). The trees I drew based on Modern Family are hard to parse. There didn’t seem to be a fool-proof system that would fit every possible family relationship in a neat way. I realized that perhaps modern families don’t fit in diagrams, and our app doesn’t have to conform to this traditional visualization.

Instead, our app would present a person’s relatives in the form of a list. A list is democratic and does not make blind assumptions about who is closer to whom.

Tapping on the items of a list would navigate to a details screen (Figure 4 right), and tapping on an Add button would present a screen for adding a new relative into the app (Figure 4 left).

Figure 4: A very rough wireframe.

Adding a relationship

Figure 5: Process of coming up with the way to define a relationship.

To represent a relationship between a new relative and the existing family members, we decided to let the user define relationship as “the ______ of someone,” like “the aunt of my mom.”

We realized that this is an imprecise way of defining a relationship. An aunt can be many things: the sister of the father, the wife of the brother of the mother, etc. As a result, we opted to force a user to define a relationship in more precise terms, such as mother, sister, and son. In Figure 5, the second screen shot is an exploration of this concept. The relationship "partner" is added in later iterations.

Screen shots 3 and 4 explore the two differing syntaxes: your grandmother can be described as your “father’s mother” or “the mother of your father.” Initially, I opted for the former, but in paper prototype testing, users found this syntax confusing, so we went with the latter.

Furthermore, we decided to use gender-neutral terms when describing relationships, which limits the number of choices to 4 instead of 8, thus decluttering the form. Another part of the form asks for the gender of the relative, and our back-end algorithm figures out the gendered title, like "female child" means "daughter." Gender-neutral terms also gives the option of a relative to identify in a non-binary gender.

Lastly, the options of “step” and “adopted/adoptive” addresses the inclusivity concerns we noticed during research. Checking one of the boxes appends the word “step,” “adopted,” or “adoptive” to the relationship name. Including these checkboxes is our effort to normalize these types of relationships that sometimes people feel ashamed about.

Colors

Considering the color scheme of the app, I looked at two different moods: a bright aqua or orange, representing modernity, or a softer palette of purples, pinks, or blues, conveying the coziness of family. I chose a pastel blue combination because the bright colors are too harsh to convey a sense of family.

Figure 6: Trying different combinations of color.

Final Design

To demonstrate the final design, I included some family members in the Tucker-Pritchett household from Modern Family. The screens in the mock-up are from the perspective of Lily, the adopted daughter of Mitchell and Cam. I also included Lily's divorced grandparents, Jay and Dede.

Implementation

I implemented the front-end features with my co-leader Lexi Ryan. We used React Native for building the interface and Redux Thunk for state management. Our back-end developers used the Flask framework in Python3. To store the data, we used a Relational Database (sqlite) with SQLAlchemy as a wrapper so it will be easy to port to psql or mysql.
